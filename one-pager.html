The greatest energy renaissance since oil has begun. Lavoit will own it.

$120/kW. Built. 20× cheaper than every incumbent on Earth. $0.74/kg hydrogen, cheaper than Saudi gas. 2 GW of LOIs signed. This is the Standard Oil of the next century.

Cost is law.

Nobody ever switched energy sources out of virtue. Kerosene killed whale oil for one reason: it was cheaper. Since Drake's well in 1859, humanity went from 1.3 billion people to 8.3 billion and GDP went up 60×, every bit of it powered by cheap molecules ripped out of the ground. Electricity is only 21% of world energy. The other 79% is molecules (fertilizer, steel, fuels, plastics), and every one of them hides the same expensive middle step: hydrogen. Whoever makes the cheapest hydrogen on Earth takes fertilizer, then steel, then chemicals, then fuel itself. Trillion-dollar markets, falling like dominoes toward the cheaper input. We make the cheapest hydrogen on Earth.

The $200 billion mess.

The hydrogen industry announced $200 billion in projects and shipped basically nothing. Hydrogen made from natural gas costs $0.7–1.9/kg. Electrolytic costs $5–7/kg. We have learnt why, because we went and looked: we walked 80%+ of the operating electrolyzer sites on Earth before we built a thing, sat the incumbent CEOs around a dinner table in Rotterdam, and got the truth: they don't understand their own machines. They inherited a century-old design built for expensive grid power ($2,400/kW, exotic catalysts, bespoke projects where EPC firms skim another 30%), and nobody has questioned any of it. It's a nine-to-five industry living off government grants. Nobody in it is trying to win.

Meanwhile solar fell 99.6% since 1976 and is still dropping 20% every capacity doubling. Cheapest electron ever produced, and electricity is 70% of hydrogen's cost. A 5% efficiency gain saves $0.15/kg. Cheap direct solar saves $2.50/kg. The lever is 17× bigger, and the incumbents physically cannot pull it: park their $2,400/kW machine on solar and their hydrogen gets more expensive ($7.60/kg), because that machine can't afford to sleep and the sun does. Winning needs a machine 30× cheaper than anything on the market. Nobody built it. So we built it.

Jörmungandr.

1 MW electrolyzer. Six weeks from first drawing to commissioned site. Four people. $120/kW all-in. Europe pays $2,400/kW. China's cheapest stripped-down machine is $540/kW. Even our test facility came in 4× under China. Because that's what Jörmungandr is: a proving ground, not a product. We built it to make our mistakes at full scale while they're still cheap to fix, and we made them: run the machine hard, find what breaks, engineer every flaw out of the next design. No miracle catalysts, no fragile lab chemistry: zero-gap alkaline, bare nickel, injection-molded plastic. Hours on a CNC machine become seconds in a mold.

The commercial machine is the 5 MW module: five identical stacks in one road-legal container, assembled by five people in under two hours, carrying every lesson Jörmungandr taught us, running in September at sub-$60/kW. Number up, don't scale up. Exactly how Ford took the Model T from $850 to $260 and buried everyone.

The empire: photon to molecule.

We don't sell hardware. Rockefeller didn't sell drills. We put our machines on the best solar coasts on Earth, own the assets, and sell the molecules. We screened the entire planet: 3,747 coastal sites in 65 countries with brutal, relentless sun: seawater in, ships out, median cost $0.83/kg. Those sites are the new oil fields, with one difference that decides the whole game: a well depletes, a factory compounds. Their costs sit on geology. Ours fall forever on a learning curve.

The $50M, 3,000 m² factory line starts construction next year. Every component we pull in-house deletes another vendor's 20% margin, and mass production cuts it again. Three by three: three technologies (electrolyzer, synthesis reactor, fuel cell) · three mass deployments (solar, machines, logistics) · three conquests (ammonia first, then methanol and olefins, then combustion itself).

The moat.

  • The incumbents have to kill themselves to follow us. Their $2,400/kW machines, doctrine, and installed base are built for expensive grid power running flat-out. Copying us means admitting the century-old machine is wrong and obsoleting their own product line. They've watched the solar curve for a decade and done nothing. Institutions don't commit suicide to chase a startup.

What you can't tear down is the process: the manufacturing process, the assembly manuals, the supplier map, the thousand decisions that turn $120/kW from a slide into a commissioned site. We built it with our own hands. That lives in the company and nowhere else.
  • The lead compounds. Every component pulled in-house deletes a vendor's 20% margin; every production doubling cuts cost again. A follower starting today starts at our year-zero cost while we ride the curve down. In manufacturing the gap between first and second never closes: Ford's didn't, ours won't.
  • Geography gets claimed once. 3,747 prime sites on Earth. First mover signs the land, the seawater, the ports, the offtake. The new oil fields get taken exactly one time.
  • At $0.74/kg, the price itself is the moat. Entering this market means beating a machine you don't have, on fields you can't get, against a cost curve that started falling years before you showed up.

Endgame.

One contract at a time, our molecule lands under theirs and the market crosses over without a speech. Nobody switches back; nobody ever does. Then energy is no longer found: it is manufactured, from sun, air, and water, priced by a learning curve instead of a cartel, falling forever. Whoever manufactures the floor of civilization owns everything built on top of it. That's us.

This is Lavoit. Molecules are not our last war. War is just the beginning.
