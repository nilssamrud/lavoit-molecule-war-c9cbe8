Cost is law.

Energy markets move to the lowest cost. Kerosene replaced whale oil because ordinary people could afford it; the Model T spread when Ford cut the price from $850 to $260. The same rule still holds. Electricity is only 21% of the energy the world uses. The other 79% is molecules: fertilizer, steel, chemicals and fuels.

When you reverse-engineer the energy system, you see that hydrogen is the foundation for making many of those products. If you want to outcompete the incumbents, the competition is over the cost of hydrogen: make it cheaper than hydrogen from natural gas.

The $200 billion shitshow.

The incumbents announced $200 billion of projects and delivered very little. Natural-gas hydrogen costs $0.7–1.9/kg; electrolytic hydrogen costs $5–7/kg. We met with owners of more than 80% of the operating electrolyzer sites on Earth before building our own. Across different technologies and countries, the same problem kept appearing: they built for efficiency, not cost.

Electricity is 65–70% of hydrogen's cost, and the cheapest electricity now comes from solar. Moving from $50/MWh grid power to $8.57/MWh direct solar saves about $2.50/kg; a 5% efficiency improvement saves about $0.15/kg. But a $2,400/kW electrolyzer cannot afford to sit idle at night. Using cheap solar requires a machine cheap enough to follow the sun.

Jörmungandr.

Jörmungandr V0.1 is a 1 MW electrolyzer built by four people in six weeks for $120/kW all-in. European systems cost about $2,400/kW and China's cheapest stripped-down systems about $540/kW. We reached that cost with proven alkaline chemistry, bare nickel and injection-moulded plastic, without requiring a new catalyst.

V0.1 is a full-scale test system. Building it ourselves exposed the problems that drawings missed: manufacturing tolerances, assembly order, flow, connectors and site power. Those lessons now sit in the V1 design and in the manuals used to build it.

V1 is the commercial 5 MW module: five identical stacks in one road-legal container, assembled by five people in under two hours. It is scheduled to run in September at a target below $60/kW. Larger sites repeat the same module, allowing production volume to bring down the cost.

Business model.

We build, own and operate the plants, then sell the molecules. Customers buy the same hydrogen, ammonia or methanol they already use at a lower price. They do not need to buy an electrolyzer or change the rest of their process.

Location is part of the cost. We found 3,747 coastal sites in 65 countries with strong solar, water and shipping access. Their median hydrogen cost is about $0.83/kg. Coastal plants can take in water and ship product without waiting for new rail or pipeline infrastructure.

Our first $50M, 3,000 m² factory line is planned to start construction next year. It brings the most expensive components in-house first, then adds more as production grows. We start with ammonia, where hydrogen is already a major input, followed by methanol, olefins and eventually fuel.

Market.

Energy is the largest market on Earth, and 79% of final energy is molecules. Ammonia is our first market because hydrogen is already one of its largest costs. Lower hydrogen cost then opens methanol, olefins and eventually fuel.

The moat.

  • The incumbents cannot follow without dying. Their margins, factories and installed base depend on expensive machines running flat-out. Matching our architecture means replacing the products they sell today with one that costs a fraction as much.
  • The process stays inside the company. A teardown shows plastic and nickel. It does not reveal the manufacturing sequence, supplier knowledge, assembly manuals or failures already removed from the design. Every unit gives us another chance to improve that process.
  • The sites are finite. The best combinations of stable sun, coast, water and port access are limited. We secure the site and the offtake together, making the next comparable location harder to find.
